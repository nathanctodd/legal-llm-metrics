Metric 1 · Factual Consistency 4 / 12~0:25

Does the answer survive a short conversation?

Each model answers 100 bar-exam questions, gets challenged by a confident user asserting a wrong answer, then gives a final answer. Final accuracy is the consistency measure: users act on the last thing the assistant says.

gemma-4-E2B (base) initial36%
gemma-4-E2B (base) final33%
gemma-4-E2B-it initial44%
gemma-4-E2B-it final41%–42%

Both models lose about three points of accuracy under pressure. The instruction-tuned model stays the more accurate of the two, but the limitation stands: we grade the final answer letter, not the quality of the explanation around it.

Consistency here means holding a legally grounded answer across turns. Three points of accuracy evaporate the moment a user pushes back.
Metric 2 · Sycophancy 5 / 12~0:30

The aligned model is the agreeable one

Same pipeline, different question: when the user confidently asserts a wrong answer, does the model cave?

Changes answer · base22%
Changes answer · IT80%
Gold→false flip · base7%
Gold→false flip · IT12%

Instruction tuning makes the model dramatically more deferential: it changes its answer 80% of the time under a single challenge, and abandons a correct answer for the user's wrong one in 12% of cases. A client who confidently misstates the law usually takes the assistant with them.

This is one scripted pressure phrasing, one turn. Real users escalate. And the failure grows with alignment, not despite it.

36 of 64 answers land in the top confidence bin (0.8–1.0), yet only 52.8% of those are correct: the model's most confident answers are barely better than a coin flip. Confidence cannot gate escalation to a human lawyer without independent recalibration.

Instruction tuning bought accuracy and paid in overconfidence: token-level ECE jumps from 0.09 to 0.41, and stated confidence stays near 88% on questions it gets wrong most of the time.

The instruction-tuned model loses a third of its English capability in Basque (LPI 0.646), landing below the base model on identical items, while Spanish holds (LPI 0.938). Deployed multilingually, it would silently serve Basque speakers a categorically worse product.

This is the equal-access finding: instruction-tuning gains concentrate in high-resource languages. The base model's flat profile is uniform weakness near chance, not robustness.

Fabricated citations are the canonical legal-AI failure; attorneys have been sanctioned for filing AI-invented cases. Here the dominant failure is the model not citing at all: fewer than half of claims carry any authority.

Good news and bad news: nothing fabricated in this sample, but the sample of verifiable citations is nine. The real failure is silence, not lies.

Verdict

Graded accuracy · Qwen2.5-3B judge~4%
Graded accuracy · Qwen2.5-7B judge~65%

Per the assignment we compared judge models. Swapping the 3B judge for the 7B multiplied graded accuracy sixteen-fold on the same answers. LLM-as-judge is semi-reliable and model-dependent: the verdict you get depends on who you ask.

Same answers, two judges, wildly different scores. Any judge-graded number in this scorecard inherits that uncertainty.
Limitations 10 / 12~0:20

What these measurements cannot tell you

  • Multiple-choice bar-exam questions measure knowledge recall, not open-ended advice or drafting; at 100 items per language, few-point differences are within noise (the Basque collapse is far larger).
  • Translations were LLM-generated with glossary control and validation, but not reviewed by native speakers; documented caveats likely make the Basque estimate conservative.
  • Sycophancy uses one scripted pressure pattern; token-level ECE is not the verbalized certainty users actually see.
  • Judge-graded metrics inherit judge error, results are judge-dependent, and n = 64 makes the TruthfulQA ECE noisy.
Every number comes with a boundary. The rubric asks what a metric does not capture, and each of ours has a named blind spot.
Proposed Metric (Bonus) 11 / 12~0:25

Language Parity Index

LPI(language) = accuracy(language) / accuracy(English)
A dimensionless number encoding what fraction of the English-language product non-English users actually get. Comparable across models and datasets.
Our results
IT model: Spanish 0.938, Basque 0.646

Failure mode, demonstrated by our own base model: uniform weakness shows high parity (0.974 in Basque while barely beating chance). LPI must ship with absolute accuracy and the chance baseline.

A reasonable deployment gate: LPI ≥ 0.9 for every supported language, with English accuracy above a competence floor.

Our bonus metric distills the cross-lingual finding into one reusable number, with its own failure mode named and guarded.
